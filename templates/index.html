What are registers?

A register is a collection of factual data that government needs in order to provide services, which meets a user need and that may be cited as the official source of truth. Example registers include: address, country, postcode, post-town, and school. Every register consists of a set of fields such as: name, text, postcode, longitude, latitude, start-date and end-date. The values that may appear in a field are constrained by its datatype such as: string, integer, decimal-degrees, and curie.

What is this alpha?

This alpha demonstrates what registers are, who they are for and how they meet their needs. This prototype is made of alpha registers exposing open data.

Who are registers for?

Developers

Developers building services within or outside of Government.

Reliable, straightforward APIs with a standardised data model enable you to build services quickly.

See demonstrations built with registers here.

Registrars

For teams that are custodians of one or more registers.

Registers help you guarantee the accuracy and validity of the data you use. You can extend registers directly into commodity caseworking and work-management systems.

Everyone

Citizens of a digital state.

With registers, private data is only collected if absolutely essential to provide a service. Private data is protected, accessed only by its attributes and, critically, never shared.

What are the benefits of registers?

  • Canonical

    Registers are canonical and data is never duplicated. Therefore, registers represent the single source of truth for platforms and services built on top of them.

  • Connected

    Where data from one register relies on data in another register, the data is not duplicated but linked, minimising redundancy and errors.

  • Standardised

    A consistent data model, and a standardised API simplifies building services using registers.

  • Trustworthy

    Registers are maintained by authorised Registrars. Ownership and responsibility for data is made clear, providing greater overall integrity and a process for redress.

  • Verifiable

    Physical-digital proofs allow users to independently validate the authenticity of registry entries.

  • Tamper-proof

    Registers are verified by the register team and developers using register-specific tests. Secure audit trails record any changes made to a register, and entries are appended to rather than edited, so the full history is preserved.

  • Open Source

    Register software is open source software - anyone can copy, verify, and contribute to the code. You can run your own copy of a public register using open data.

This alpha is being developed by a small team at the Government Digital Service. We welcome contributions and feedback.
